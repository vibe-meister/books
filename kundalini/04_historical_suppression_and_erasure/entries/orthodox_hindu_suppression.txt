**ORTHODOX HINDU SUPPRESSION OF TANTRIC PRACTICES**

Modern definition:
Systematic suppression of Tantric and kundalini-related practices by orthodox Hindu authorities from approximately the 8th century CE onwards, who labeled these practices as "heterodox," "heretical," or "debased," leading to the destruction of texts, persecution of practitioners, and forced underground transmission of knowledge.

Historical roots:
As Tantric traditions developed from the 5th–8th centuries CE, they came into conflict with orthodox Brahmanical Hinduism, which emphasized Vedic ritual, caste hierarchy, and textual authority. Orthodox authorities, particularly those aligned with Advaita Vedanta and other "respectable" schools, systematically suppressed Tantric practices, which they saw as challenging established social and religious order. This suppression intensified during periods of Islamic rule (12th–18th centuries) when orthodox Hindus sought to distance themselves from practices that might be seen as "idolatrous" or "superstitious" by Muslim rulers.

Ancient names & spellings:
- Orthodox term for Tantra: वाममार्ग (vāmamārga) - "left-hand path" (pejorative)
- Heterodox: अपसिद्धान्त (apasiddhānta) - "wrong doctrine"
- Heretical: पाषण्ड (pāṣaṇḍa) - "heretic"
- Orthodox position: स्मार्त (smārta) - "traditionalist"

How it was lost:
Orthodox suppression of Tantric and kundalini knowledge occurred through: (1) Systematic destruction of Tantric texts and manuscripts, (2) Persecution and execution of Tantric practitioners, (3) Banning of Tantric practices from "respectable" Hindu practice, (4) Forcing knowledge underground into secret lineages, (5) Deliberate exclusion of Tantric texts from canonical collections, and (6) Creating negative associations that persist to this day, making it difficult for authentic knowledge to be transmitted openly.

Ties to the book:
Orthodox Hindu suppression represents one of the earliest and most effective mechanisms for erasing kundalini knowledge, demonstrating how religious authority can systematically eliminate practices that challenge established power structures, even within the same religious tradition.

THE ORTHODOX-TANTRIC CONFLICT

The conflict between orthodox and Tantric traditions had multiple dimensions:

PHILOSOPHICAL DIFFERENCES:

ORTHODOX POSITION:
- Emphasis on Vedic authority
- Textual and ritual focus
- Caste hierarchy essential
- Renunciation and asceticism
- Transcendence of world

TANTRIC POSITION:
- Direct experience emphasis
- Practical techniques
- Challenge to caste restrictions
- Integration of world
- Transformation through engagement

SOCIAL CHALLENGES:

Tantric practices challenged orthodox social order:
- Women could be teachers and practitioners
- Caste restrictions were transcended
- Direct access to spiritual experience
- Challenge to priestly authority
- Empowerment of individuals

RELIGIOUS AUTHORITY:

Tantric practices threatened orthodox authority:
- Direct experience vs. priestly mediation
- Personal practice vs. ritual dependence
- Individual transformation vs. institutional control
- Accessible techniques vs. restricted knowledge

MECHANISMS OF SUPPRESSION

Orthodox authorities used multiple methods:

TEXTUAL DESTRUCTION:
- Burning of Tantric manuscripts
- Exclusion from canonical collections
- Deliberate loss of texts
- Suppression of written knowledge

PERSECUTION OF PRACTITIONERS:
- Execution of "heretics"
- Social ostracism
- Economic sanctions
- Physical violence

BANNING OF PRACTICES:
- Prohibition of Tantric rituals
- Exclusion from temples
- Ban on public practice
- Forced underground transmission

NEGATIVE PROPAGANDA:
- Labeling as "debased"
- Association with "black magic"
- Sexualization and demonization
- Creating fear and rejection

INSTITUTIONAL EXCLUSION:
- Ban from orthodox institutions
- Exclusion from education
- Denial of religious authority
- Marginalization of traditions

HISTORICAL PERIODS OF INTENSIFIED SUPPRESSION

SHANKARA'S REFORM (8th–9th centuries CE):
- Advaita Vedanta establishment
- Emphasis on "pure" philosophy
- Rejection of "debased" practices
- Textual authority over experience

ISLAMIC RULE PERIOD (12th–18th centuries):
- Orthodox distancing from "idolatry"
- Suppression to avoid Muslim persecution
- Hiding of practices
- Further marginalization

BRITISH COLONIAL PERIOD (18th–19th centuries):
- Orthodox collaboration with colonizers
- Presentation of "respectable" Hinduism
- Further suppression of "superstition"
- Academic dismissal

MODERN PERIOD (20th–21st centuries):
- Continued marginalization
- Association with "New Age"
- Academic bias persists
- Difficulty in authentic transmission

THE "LEFT-HAND PATH" STIGMA

Orthodox authorities created the "left-hand" vs. "right-hand" distinction:

RIGHT-HAND PATH (DAKSHINAMARGA):
- "Respectable" practices
- Orthodox approval
- Socially acceptable
- Textual and ritual focus

LEFT-HAND PATH (VAMAMARGA):
- "Debased" practices
- Orthodox condemnation
- Socially unacceptable
- Direct experience focus

This distinction was used to:
- Marginalize Tantric practices
- Create negative associations
- Justify suppression
- Maintain social control

TEXTUAL LOSS:

- How suppression works

The orthodox Hindu suppression of Tantric and kundalini practices represents a crucial historical mechanism for knowledge loss. Understanding this suppression is essential for appreciating both what was lost and the challenges involved in recovery. The patterns of suppression—textual destruction, persecution, marginalization, and stigma—continue to affect how kundalini knowledge is understood and transmitted today. Recovery efforts require both textual reconstruction and the rebuilding of authentic transmission lineages while overcoming centuries of marginalization and bias.
