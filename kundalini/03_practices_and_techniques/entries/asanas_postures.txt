**ASANAS: POSTURES FOR KUNDALINI**

Modern definition:
Asanas are physical postures described in classical yoga texts that prepare the body, open energy channels, balance the nervous system, and create the physical foundation necessary for safe kundalini awakening. While modern yoga often reduces asanas to exercise, classical texts describe them as essential preparation for energy work.

Historical roots:
Asanas are first systematically described in the Hatha Yoga Pradipika (15th century CE), which lists 15 primary postures, though the text states that Shiva taught 8.4 million asanas. Earlier references appear in the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali (c. 200 BCE–400 CE), which mentions asana as one of the eight limbs of yoga but provides few details. The Gheranda Samhita (17th century CE) describes 32 asanas. Classical texts emphasize that asanas prepare the body for pranayama and energy work, not as ends in themselves.

Ancient names & spellings:
- Sanskrit: आसन (āsana) - "seat," "posture," "position"
- Key asanas:
  - सिद्धासन (siddhāsana) - accomplished pose
  - पद्मासन (padmāsana) - lotus pose
  - स्वस्तिकासन (svastikāsana) - auspicious pose
  - वीरासन (vīrāsana) - hero pose

How it was lost:
The deeper purpose and techniques of asanas were lost because: (1) Modern yoga reduced asanas to physical exercise, stripping away their energetic and spiritual dimensions, (2) The specific techniques for using asanas to open energy channels and prepare for kundalini were kept in oral transmission and lost when lineages were disrupted, (3) The integration of asanas with pranayama, mudra, and bandha for energy work was largely forgotten, and (4) British colonial and modern interpretations focused on physical benefits while ignoring the subtle body and energy work aspects.

Ties to the book:
Asanas demonstrate how practical techniques can survive in reduced form while their deeper purpose and integration with energy work are lost, showing both preservation and loss occurring simultaneously.

THE CLASSICAL UNDERSTANDING OF ASANAS

Classical texts describe asanas very differently from modern yoga:

DEFINITION:
- "Asana is that which is steady and comfortable" (Yoga Sutras)
- Stability and ease, not flexibility or strength
- Foundation for other practices
- Preparation for energy work

PURPOSE:
- Prepare physical body
- Open energy channels (nadis)
- Balance nervous system
- Create stability for pranayama
- Foundation for kundalini work

INTEGRATION:
- Not separate from other practices
- Works with pranayama, mudra, bandha
- Part of complete system
- Preparation, not goal

THE PRIMARY ASANAS FOR KUNDALINI

Classical texts emphasize specific asanas for energy work:

SIDDHASANA (ACCOMPLISHED POSE):

Description:
- Sit with one heel at perineum
- Other heel above it
- Hands in specific mudra
- Spine erect
- Eyes closed or focused

Purpose:
- Activates mūlādhāra chakra
- Prepares for kundalini awakening
- Opens energy channels
- Creates stability for practice

Effects:
- Grounds energy
- Prepares base chakra
- Creates foundation
- Supports awakening process

PADMASANA (LOTUS POSE):

Description:
- Cross-legged with feet on opposite thighs
- Hands in mudra
- Spine erect
- Balanced and stable

Purpose:
- Opens all energy channels
- Balances ida and pingala
- Prepares sushumna
- Foundation for advanced work

Effects:
- Creates stability
- Opens channels
- Balances energy
- Supports integration

SVASTIKASANA (AUSPICIOUS POSE):

Description:
- Cross-legged with feet tucked
- Simpler than lotus
- Stable and comfortable
- Accessible alternative

Purpose:
- Preparation pose
- Opens channels
- Creates stability
- Accessible for many

Effects:
- Grounding
- Channel opening
- Stability
- Foundation building
